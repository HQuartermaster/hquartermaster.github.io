# Societal Aspiration and the Sino-Indian Divergence

![Moscow, Bukharin's USSR, Year 100 of the NEP](div/bukharin.png)

_Moscow, Bukharin's USSR, Year 100 of the NEP._

It's that time of the year when China-India comparisons are happening again, this time due to an article from substack.

I won't go much into the article specifically, but one less talked about thing amongst these articles that compare the growth trajectories of different nations is an idea what I've been getting around, I call it "societal aspiration". 

Societal aspiration is the general direction a society wants to head in. It is the society's "blue print for the future". This is ultimately downstream of two things: political ideology and culture (via films, TV shows, songs, literature etc.)

In my opinion (and you can disagree), the most successful part about Chinese Communism was injecting Soviet futurist societal aspiration into Chinese society. The Soviet Union's "vision for the future" had them reach high up in space, build machines that kept NATO in awe and always had them chasing the bleeding edge. The idea of progress was the pursuit of utopia, and utopia could be delivered by technology and institutions. 

Sino-Futurism was a thing even when China was poor. You can find Chinese propaganda posters celebrating the country's locomotive and steel industry even from the 1960s, you cannot find the same for India. Even so many years later, when the Indian steel industry is now the second largest in the world. Simply put, Indians do not think like that because their societal aspiration does not care for steel produced per year.

India has capabilities that put it in the Big Four (US, Russia, China and India), it has a nuclear weapons & reactor program, it has a fleet of locally designed and manufactured space launch vehicles, it operates nuclear submarines and aircraft carriers, it is amongst the only four nations known to possess the capability to strike satellites in orbit, and yet...it suffers from a total lack of a "futurist" aspiration. The reason why these exist is not because India has an aspiration to dominate like the US or Russia, they exist because India is surrounded by Pakistan and China, two nuclear powers themselves.

If India was in a more geographically secure position it would not embark on these strategic programs. The point is, India is forced to invest in strategic projects. It has no aspiration of its own. It acts, for the most part, in a purely reactive manner; and much of this can be ascribed to Indian politics lacking a vision for the future.

Science fiction is rare in Indian cinema. In fact, it is non-existent. The good Hindi films are period movies set in warring eras of medieval India and murder mysteries. This complete absence of sci-fi has downstream effects on society. The thing about science fiction is that it is stimulating for the mind, it makes you imagine possibilities that do not exist in real life, the pursuit of which can lead to great things. There was more Indian science fiction in 11th century CE than today, for example: a famous Sanskrit play, the _Kathasaritsagara_, imagines a city filled with machinations that serve the people, mechanical swans made from strings and pulleys that fly to steal the King's treasure. The Indian of today however is a luddite in most aspects.

China, in contrast, has a very vibrant culture of futuristic military novels. Chinese users make lots of animated videos (that are rather decently made) showing future possible conflicts with the US featuring military equipment. In fact, amongst the most famous online Chinese military novels, called "National Will", features a war between India and Pakistan where China saves the day for Pakistan and India is defeated. The novel is praised for its attention to detail of military equipment involved in the conflict. The Three Body Problem is now a globally acclaimed science fiction novel. 

You may think that "hey, it's just a bunch of young kids playing video games", but it was discovered a few months ago that the PLA was running CFD / Solid Dynamics simulations of Chinese missiles hitting US aircraft carriers and the files got leaked. You cannot ignore the influence of culture.

What is _Indian societal aspiration_ then? Because India is a democracy, the political notion of progress differs. For some, it is bringing in affirmative action in private sector, for some it is creating _Hindu Rashtra_, for some it is _Azaadi_ (freedom), for some it is the member of their caste becoming the Chief Minister of a state, for some it is moving to the US, for some...you see the problem? There is no section of Indian society (aside from a few posters on X perhaps) that actually has a vision for the future.   

Every explanation that Indians come up with is relating to one of caste, or religion or something "cultural", when most of India's problems are relating to lack of resources and institutional capability! 

Here is a line of thinking: India has the third largest deficit on the planet, after US & UK. US & UK are advanced economies, India is still a developing country. This deficit causes shrinkage of the rupee, and its cause is structural: India is a heavy importer of crude oil, semiconductors, and military equipment. This import dependency is a massive reason why Indians are poor.

And yet there is little to no discussion in popular Indian zeitgeist. Even though it affects all of us. Everyday. 

Imagine if we found a source of crude, somewhere in Andaman, or something. Imagine if we stopped importing $100 billion+ worth of aircrafts and instead spent money into our own aircraft programs. Imagine the jobs that'd be created from that, welders, technicians, R&D staff, upper and lower classes alike. Imagine the technology we'd have in our hands, it'd be ours and we could iterate it as much as we wanted. Imagine if we spent a lot more on our space programme and launch capability. We could put thousands (as opposed to tens to hundreds) of satellites from weather monitoring, to navigation to military applications. We could predict weather patterns with far greater accuracy. At some point, we could probably deploy Alcubierran warp drive engines carrying a ship full of artificially manufactured but sentient future human(oids) to reach and settle Kepler-452b. Well, it's never too little to dream! 

Now, I don't think sitting and dreaming of Alcubierran warp drives or Dyson spheres, or artificial qualia will lead to economic growth; but a higher sense of aspiration will go a long way to formulate policies that make sense. Currently, our aspirations are poor. Our strategic aspirations are purely reactive. We think importing Rafales are an achievement. Economic growth is heavily intertwined with geopolitics, and geopolitical equations depend upon a country's hard power which are a downstream of the country's societal aspiration and industries.

What is my vision for the future? I hope in 2070 there will be a 19 year old boarding a BEML HRH-580 trainset going back to his home from college, and on the way back home, he'd see this massive concrete structure, that could be seen from 50 km away, glowing in the distance with the letters HSI (Human Spaceflight International), a company specializing in launch vehicles that work on logistics and transportation to the Moon as he says to his himself "Man, I wish I worked there", as the HRH-580 speeds up to its maximum operating speed of 560 km/h, moving downwards into the longest tunnel on Earth, operating at a vacuum of a 3 psi, that ought to be built between the cities of Delhi and Mumbai. He'd then see a massive 32-lane road, and see a couple making out in the frontseat of a MEM (Modern Electronic Motors) X-370 speeding to 300 km/h, breaking the lane speed limit of 280 km/h and immediately feel jealous. He would then receive a call from his grandmother who recently got diagnosed with pancreatic cancer 2 months ago and was injected with SuperSun Pancrion-X and is doing fine now with tumours gone, just maybe a little weak, it is no longer a death sentence. You can laugh, but fuck man, I'd be ashamed if we didn't achieve a fraction of that by 2070.
