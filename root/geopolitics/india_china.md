# The India-China Parity That Never Was

![India-China Comparison Covers](/india-china/comparison.png)

There is a particular genre of tweets and a narrative that pisses me off consistently. Not because I am a big fan of the Modi administration (I'm not), but rather it is a factually wrong, and a strategically blunderous take on the China-India disparity.

The narrative goes like this: 

> In the 2000s, Western newspapers and magazines constantly depicted the comparison between India and China...how it was the "showdown of the century"...how two Asian giants were catching up economically...and fast forward to now...Indian economy has been ruined[sic], China has fancy AI gadgets, it has manufacturing, it is building fifth generation fighter jets! Modi ruined India! We are now hyphenated with Pakistan!

First of all, this assumes journos know what they are talking about. As we have discovered over the last five years, they do not. They are, by and large, a group of arts and political science majors that got their positions via nepotism or social connections. 

Here's another take you get to hear once in a while:

> Until 1990 (or whatever year the dataset says, usually around that), India had a higher GDP per capita than China[@indiachinagdppc]! Then, (Insert statement supporting ideology from libertarianism to IQ hereditarianism).

But this is also not true.

**The India-China Parity *never* existed** in the modern day. 

The last time India-China parity existed was, perhaps in 1500 CE. India had integrated gunpowder well into warfare unlike China at the time. At the same time, India was also a producer of high quality wootz steel, and had better artillery casting methods. But China made for the lack of this by having a more centralized bureaucracy and military. Indian armies at the time were, after all, raised by a local Zamindar, composed heavily of peasant levies with a minority of tactically trained units. But we'll talk about that in another article.

## A History Lesson

A lot of Indians are very unaware of modern history of the People's Republic of China, so let me just rewind the critical parts:

China unlike India, was a full sovereign power in World War II. It got to sit on the table with the "big boys" of the time: the USSR, the United States, the UK, and France, creating the UNSC P5 - "Power of Five". This structure continues to the modern day, where only these five nations have veto power in the United Nations.

Even before World War II, China was growing at an immense pace. The era of the 1930s was dubbed "the Nanjing Decade"[@nanjingdecade]. Even Anglo-Saxon administered India could not catch up with Chinese growth. That said, Anglo-Saxon intent to administer India for prosperity of its inhabitants was questionable at best. But that is besides the point. It is very likely that if the Japanese Invasion of China had not occurred in 1937, China would have caught up with the Western world much sooner than anticipated. 

Right after the People's Republic of China :flag[cn] overthrew the Republic of China :flag[tw], it immediately embarked upon a war with the United States: the Korean War[@koreanwar]. The war was initially considered lost by the DPRK :flag[kp] as the UN/ROK :flag[kr] forces had managed to reach the Yalu River[@yaluriver], almost conquering the entirety of North Korea until China entered the war and ultimately pushed the Americans back to the 38th Parallel[@thirtyeigthparallel]. During this war, the Soviet Union greatly aided the Chinese, and this war increased the prestige of China immensely. It had, after all, gone against the primary great power at the time...and secured parity decisively! Sure, we can talk about how South Korea outpaced North Korea's economic growth etc. etc., but securing parity with the US was a victory for the newly established People's Republic of China. It enforced the legitimacy of the regime. It also increased China's strategic heft and heavy Soviet technology transfers flooded in. China quickly began making copies of Soviet aircraf, artillery, missiles, and modifying them to suit its needs.

## The "India UNSC offer"

Now, with all this knowledge you've been armed with, I'll proceed to debunk another common myth in Indian political circles: 

> Nehru ceded UNSC permanent seat to China [@nehruunscchina]. The US agreed to give a permanent seat to India to be in place of China.

This was never happening! China's strategic heft (as shown in the previous section) had been established. India was not an equal; China had established itself as a Tier-1 power. India was a Tier-2 power. Even if the US agreed, the USSR would never. India was already tending towards USSR due to its geopolitical position on colonialism. It was simply not possible. In English, the phrase is, "a pipe dream"[@pipedream].

## What The Data Says

So, how can we measure the India-China disparity? If not GDP per capita, then what? 

GDP per capita is a useful metric, but historical comparisons between India and China-especially pre-China's entry into the WTO [@chinawto] can be misleading. The issue is that China’s post-1978 market reforms and its 2001 WTO accession created major discontinuities in growth and data comparability, while India, had earlier GATT (precursor to WTO) membership, despite remaining a closed economy until its 1991 liberalization. As a result, pre-reform GDP per capita figures for both countries reflect very different economic systems and measurement conditions, that limits direct comparison.

What can we compare, then? What can be a good indicator of economic growth and industrial progress? I propose and the reasons can be self-evident, the following:

- Female Literacy Rate [@flr]
- Infant Mortality Rate (IMR) [@imr]
- Electricity Generation[@electricitygen]
- Steel Production[@worldsteel]
- Cement Production[@chinacement] [@indiacement] [@cementwiki]

Yes, these are very "bare bones" industrial metrics; but they are quantities that can be directly compared.

## Is it over?

> Until death, all defeat is psychological.

It can feel like a barrage of blackpill when you are first confronted with this data. At least I did. Because when I was a kid, I used to always think that China was a country that was similar to ours. Similar populations, similar grind and love of bureaucratic and competitive examinations JEE-Gaokao, UPSC-Guokao, they were considered to be a Buddhist country and Buddhism has of course, quite a bit of parallels with Hinduism, etc., etc., you get the point. Of course, many Indians thought the same. It was not until the internet came to India when the first sign of the disparity was beginning to be realised. Indians got to see the videos of Chinese cities, of Chinese technology, of Chinese industrial parks, of Chinese universities and colleges. In a world where the public perception of China was from the Hindi film, *Chandini Chowk to China*, this was different. Many thought that China had "leapfrogged recently" and this view continues to permeate the Indian space.

But as we have shown, this is a wrong view. And it was borderline delusional of us to think that we were at parity with China at any point in time. In fact, now we know that Chinese strategic restraint (or 'cowardice', if you are feeling a little brave today) is what caused this delusion to appear. That because the Chinese were not bombing the shit out of us at the border that we were somehow equals. 

But Chinese strategic restraint has a clock. The newer generations of Chinese are going to be far more aggressive. They will not have the sympathy towards India that even Mao once had, quoted, "India is a great country and the Indian people are a great people. The Chinese and Indian people are old friends and they cannot always quarrel" [@maoindia]. Instead, the newer Chinese are going to view Indians with far more disdain than the old ones. The best parallel comes from Imperial Japan. The Imperial Japanese upon Japan's advancement against Asian powers, saw themselves as a superior race to the rest of Asia especially China. Imperial Japanese rhetoric called China "a fake country", and said that "Japan should take whatever parts of China it pleases".[@imperialjapanview] This view is not uncommon of India amongst many younger Chinese today. One of the most popular Chinese military novels, if not the most popular, is about a future India-Pakistan War where China enters and militarily crushes India titled National Will[@nationalwill].

I can only say this: deterrence is the only way to stop any misadventures in Asia. True deterrance. Not running away like cowards to seek shelter under the Americans. The Filipinos, Japanese, Koreans, maybe even Taiwanese could do that. We cannot. A tiger, even if wounded, shitting itself and bleeding, cannot seek refuge in a lion's den. Moreover, a lion is not amicable to tiger, and the Americans have no interest in the defence of a "brown" "pagan" nation like India. 

It is time we abandon critical theory, decoloniality, and other luddite ideologies, and embrace industrialism. Improvements in military technology and industry are the only thing that is going to stop a J-36[@j36] flying over your house while a YJ-10[@cj10] zeroes in on your child's school.
